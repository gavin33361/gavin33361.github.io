Logical Operations

Logical operations are often called Boolean operations, in honor of George Boole, a nineteenth century mathematician and philosopher, who was the first to systematically study such logical operations.

The simplest operations are unary, meaning that they only operate upon one item (or operand). The most important unary logical operation is logical negation, often called the NOT operation. Logical negation turns true into false and false into true.

We denote the (logical) negation of a logical variable by p by p'. The value of the NOT operation is given by the following truth table:

pp'
TF
FT

In a truth table, we use "T" and "F" to respectively denote "true" and "false". The first row of the truth table identifies the variable name p and the value of p' of the Boolean operation. The remaining rows tell us how to compute logical negation:

When p = T, we have p' = F, i.e, T' = F.

When p = F, we have p' = T, i.e, F' = T.

Binary logical operations mean that the logical operations have two operands. There are two basic binary operations:

Logical conjunction, also called the AND operation, and denoted by the symbol ∧. The conjunction is true if and only if both of its operands are true.

Logical disjunction, also called the (inclusive) OR operation, and denoted by the symbol ∨. The disjunction is true if and only if either of its operands are true.

We can summarize these two operations in the following truth tables:

pqp ∧ q
TTT
TFF
FTF
FFF
pqp ∨ q
TTT
TFT
FTT
FFF

p ∨ q is true even when both p and q are true. This is not the same as "either-or." The exclusive or, often called the XOR operation and denoted by ⊕. The truth table of XOR is given by

pqp ⊕ q
TTF
TFT
FTT
FFF

Conditional operation, denoted by ⇒, is often referred to as implication or even conditional implication. It can be read in several different ways:

If p, then q.

p implies q.

p only if q.

p is sufficient for q.

q is necessary for p.

The truth table for conditional operation is:

pqp ⇒ q
TTT
TFF
FTT
FFT

Biconditional operation, denoted by ⇔, is often referred to as biconditional implication. It can be read in several different ways:

p if and only if q.

p is necessary and sufficient for q.

p is logically equivalent to q.

The truth table for biconditional operation is:

pqp ⇔ q
TTT
TFF
FTF
FFT
